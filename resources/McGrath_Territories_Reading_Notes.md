# Reading Notes: Alister E. McGrath, *The Territories of Human Reason: Science and Theology in an Age of Multiple Rationalities* (Oxford: OUP, 2019)

**Prepared for:** Prof. Michal Valčo | **Purpose:** Extract ideas for (a) a script on Christian philosophy of science in Buddhist context, and (b) reference notes for round-table discussion.

---

## I. The Core Thesis — and Why It Matters for Our Project

McGrath's central argument is deceptively simple: there is no single universal rationality. What exists instead is a plurality of "multiple situated rationalities" — domain-specific, culturally embedded, historically shaped ways of reasoning that arise organically within distinct epistemic communities. The natural sciences themselves operate with a plurality of methods and criteria, not one monolithic "Scientific Method."

This is *precisely* the epistemological space our project needs. If rationality were truly universal and singular — the Enlightenment dream — then any East-West philosophical dialogue would inevitably become a contest: whose rationality wins? But McGrath (drawing on Wittgenstein, MacIntyre, Bhaskar, and others) shows that this framing is itself a historically contingent artifact of Western modernity, not a necessary truth.

**The formula that runs through the book** (borrowed from biologist Steven Rose): We live in a world that is an *ontological unity*, but must accept an *epistemological pluralism* in investigating it.

> This is the conceptual key for our Buddhism-Christianity-Natural Science conversation. The world is one. Our methods for engaging it are — and must be — many.


## II. Key Ideas for the Script / Lecture

### 1. The "Buddhism Problem" in Defining Religion (Introduction, pp. 10–11)

McGrath explicitly engages Buddhism as a test case that breaks Western definitions of religion. He cites Jong's analysis: Western scholarly efforts to define "religion" face two difficulties — the "Buddhism Problem" (definitions based on belief in God/gods exclude Buddhism) and the "Football Problem" (definitions based on meaning-making functions include things like fanatical sports fandom). 

McGrath's point: "religion" is a *social construction*, not a natural type. It cannot be defined scientifically. Dennett's claim that "a religion without God or gods is like a vertebrate without a backbone" is exposed as a parochial American cultural assumption.

**For our script:** This opens the door to taking Buddhism seriously on its own terms, not as a deficient form of theistic religion, but as a distinct epistemic tradition with its own rationality. It also allows us to position Christian theology as one tradition among several — without surrendering the claim that its particular "big picture" has unique explanatory depth.

---

### 2. Rationality as Embodied and Embedded (Ch. 1, pp. 22–25)

McGrath argues (drawing on embodied cognition research) that *how we think* is shaped by three factors:

- **Natural cognitive processes** (largely culture-independent)
- **Prevailing cultural metanarratives** (which shape what counts as "reasonable" or "common sense")
- **Available evidence** (which varies by cultural and historical location)

Rationality is not an abstract faculty floating above history. It is exercised within a *Lebensform* and a *Lebenswelt* (Wittgenstein). What seems self-evidently rational in one cultural location may appear irrational in another — and vice versa.

**For our script:** This is essential for framing the Buddhist-Christian encounter. A Buddhist monk reasoning about *śūnyatā* and a Christian theologian reasoning about the *imago Dei* are both exercising rationality — but within radically different metanarratives. Neither can be judged by the other's standards as "irrational" without committing a category error. The task is *correlation*, not adjudication.

---

### 3. Against Scientism (Ch. 2, pp. 56–59)

McGrath, drawing on Ian Kidd, identifies three "impulses" behind scientism:

- **Imperialist urge** — extending scientific methods into domains where their competency is "uncalibrated and almost certainly problematic"
- **Salvific urge** — insisting that science can satisfy ethical, spiritual, and existential needs
- **Absolutist urge** — assigning science the exclusive task of providing totalizing accounts

McGrath demonstrates that scientism is trapped in a vicious circle: it makes *philosophical* claims about the authority of science that cannot themselves be verified scientifically. As Mary Midgley puts it, scientism's error is not in over-praising science, but in "cutting that form off from the rest of thought, treating it as a victor who has put all the rest out of business."

**For our script / round table:** This section is directly relevant to the AI ethics dimension. The claim that AI can replicate (or surpass) human consciousness rests on tacit scientistic assumptions — that the measurable, computable, and optimizable exhaust what is real about the human person. McGrath's critique provides philosophical ammunition for challenging this.

---

### 4. Ontological Unity + Epistemological Pluralism: The Frog Fable (Ch. 2, pp. 59–60)

McGrath uses Steven Rose's "fable of five biologists" who observe a frog jump into a pond. Each gives a different explanation: the physiologist (muscle impulses), the biochemist (protein mechanics), the developmental biologist (ontogenetic processes), the animal behaviourist (escape from a snake), and the evolutionary biologist (natural selection). Rose's point: "All five explanations are part of a bigger picture. All of them are right; they are, however, *different*."

The challenge is moving from the recognition of multiple perspectives to a unified theoretical account — without reducing any perspective to another.

**For our project:** This is a powerful analogy for what we're attempting with Buddhism, Christianity, and natural science. Each offers a genuine perspective on human nature, consciousness, and meaning. The task is *colligation* (see below), not elimination.

---

### 5. Distinct Levels of Reality / Critical Realism (Ch. 2, pp. 65–68)

Drawing on Roy Bhaskar's critical realism, McGrath argues that reality is *stratified*. Each stratum demands its own investigative method. Physics is fundamental; chemistry builds on physics while extending it in unpredictable ways; biology extends both further. Crucially, "bottom-up" causation must be supplemented by "top-down" mechanisms: "physics does not control the mind, it *enables* the mind."

Thomas F. Torrance's parallel argument: every intellectual discipline must engage reality "according to its distinct nature" — the object determines the method, not the other way around.

**For our project:** This framework supports the claim that theology engages a real stratum of reality (meaning, purpose, transcendence) that is not reducible to the strata studied by natural science. It also provides space for Buddhist engagement with consciousness as operating at its own level.

---

### 6. Mystery: Trans-Rational, Not Irrational (Ch. 7, pp. 182–201)

This chapter is one of the richest in the book. Key moves:

- **Rudolf Otto's "numinous"** is not irrational but *non-rational* or *trans-rational* — something that overwhelms and saturates rational capacities rather than contradicting them.
- **Ancient Greek philosophy** was never "mere rationalism" — even the first European rationalists were "deeply and imaginatively aware of the power, the wonder and the peril of the Irrational."
- **Awe and vastness:** Recent psychological research shows that the experience of awe arises from the mind's fundamental inability to accommodate vast phenomena — the night sky, physical landscapes, *intellectual systems such as Christianity*.
- **Richard Feynman's observation:** The imagination is "stretched to the utmost, not... to imagine things which are not really there, but just to comprehend those things which *are* there."
- **Max Weber's *stahlhartes Gehäuse*** ("shell as hard as steel"): Rationalism can become an intellectual prison, confining us to only what can be proven "rational."
- **Giambattista Vico's insight:** The human mind can fully grasp only what humans have *constructed*; both nature and God lie beyond human creation, and therefore both natural science and theology must struggle for even *partial* comprehension.

**For our project:** This section is gold for the Buddhist-Christian dialogue. Both traditions have sophisticated understandings of what lies beyond rational articulation — the apophatic tradition in Christianity, the concept of *śūnyatā* in Buddhism, the experience of *satori* in Zen. McGrath's framework dignifies this shared recognition of the limits of discursive reason without collapsing into irrationalism.

---

### 7. Colligation — The Key Methodological Concept (Ch. 8, pp. 210–212)

McGrath recovers William Whewell's concept of "colligation" — an act of thought that brings together empirical facts by "superinducing" upon them a connecting idea, "in much the same way as a string holds together the pearls of a necklace."

But McGrath extends Whewell: colligation here means constructing a "big picture" capable of accommodating and interconnecting insights from *across intellectual disciplines*. Critically:

- Colligation does **not** resolve or depend on resolving the methodological asymmetry between disciplines
- Each discipline *retains its own distinctive identity*
- Colligation is a **matter of choice**, reflecting personal or communal epistemic conclusions — not compelled by publicly unassailable evidence
- It involves making **connections at different levels of analysis** — functional explanation on one hand, meaning/value on the other

**For our project:** "Colligation" is exactly the word — and the method — we need for what we're doing. We are not merging Buddhism, Christianity, and natural science into some syncretistic whole. We are colligating their insights, allowing each to retain its identity while exploring what larger picture emerges from their juxtaposition. This should feature prominently in any lecture or publication.

---

### 8. Science and Theology as Non-Competing Perspectives (Ch. 8, pp. 218–221)

McGrath's central distinction: science and theology are *different*, but they are not competing for the same explanatory territory. The mistake (Frazer, Dennett) is to treat religion as a primitive form of science; doing so forces them into competition, when in reality they address different questions and operate at different levels.

McGrath then offers a remarkable test case on *human nature*, showing how three insights from different disciplines can be colligated:

1. From evolutionary biology: humans are genetically predisposed to selfish behaviour
2. From cultural anthropology: humans are predisposed to use narratives for identity and meaning
3. From Christian theology: humans are sinful, alienated from God, and in need of salvation

These can be "woven together through allowing the Christian narrative of sin and the scientific narrative of evolution to inform and critique each other." This interweaving *respects their distinct identities* while encouraging cross-fertilization.

**For our project:** Replace (or supplement) point 3 with a Buddhist counterpart — e.g., "humans are trapped in *saṃsāra*, driven by *taṇhā* (craving), and in need of *nibbāna* (liberation)" — and you have a model for exactly the kind of three-way colligation we're developing. The structure of the argument is transferable.

---

### 9. Trinitarian "Meta-Rationality" (Ch. 8, pp. 224–225)

Near the end, McGrath makes a suggestive but undeveloped proposal: a Trinitarian "big picture" could function as a *meta-rationality* — a framework capable of accommodating other religious traditions and providing the scaffolding for grasping the relation of sciences and theology. He is careful to note that this meta-rationality is *specific to the Christian tradition* and carries little epistemic weight beyond its boundaries, but for Christian thinkers it provides a "robust foundation and framework for transdisciplinary conversations."

**For our project:** This is where McGrath's argument most directly supports the Christian Personalist approach. The *imago Dei*, the relational ontology of the Trinity, and the doctrine of creation provide the canvas on which to paint the larger picture — not as neutral ground (there is none), but as the specific standpoint from which a Christian researcher engages Buddhist and scientific interlocutors. Honesty about standpoint is more productive than pretense of neutrality.

---

### 10. Wisdom as "Deeper Rationality" (Ch. 8, p. 225)

McGrath closes by citing E.O. Wilson's observation: "We are drowning in information, while starving for wisdom." But wisdom, McGrath argues, is not mere knowledge — it is "a deeper rationality which represents the integration of insights, and the application of these to real life." It is about *colligating* facts and "grasping and enacting their coherence-making and meaning-generating properties."

**For our project:** This is the ultimate horizon of the Buddhism-Christianity-Science dialogue. We are not merely after academic publications. We are after *wisdom* — the kind of integrated understanding that enables human flourishing in an age of technological disruption.

## III. Potential Direct Uses

### For the Lecture / Script:
- Use McGrath's "ontological unity + epistemological pluralism" as the epistemological framework
- Open with the "Buddhism Problem" to show that standard Western categories already break down
- Introduce "colligation" as the method for the interdisciplinary project
- Use the "five biologists and the frog" as an accessible illustration
- Frame AI ethics critique through the anti-scientism argument
- Close with the wisdom/deeper rationality theme

### For the Round Table:
- The question of "multiple situated rationalities" is itself a conversation starter — invite Buddhist and Vietnamese colleagues to reflect on whether Western philosophy of science adequately describes *their* forms of reasoning
- The Mystery chapter provides common ground: both traditions recognize what lies beyond discursive articulation
- Bhaskar's critical realism as a shared framework that doesn't privilege any single tradition

### For Future Publications:
- McGrath's framework could structure an entire article on the methodology of the Buddhism-Christianity-Science project
- The human nature colligation (selfish genes + narrative identity + sin/craving) is a ready-made comparative schema
- The Trinitarian meta-rationality section could be developed into a full argument for a Christian philosophy of interreligious dialogue about science
