# Buddhism & Christianity: Convergences and Divergences
## Scholarly Reference File

**Prepared for:** Prof. Michal Valčo | **Project:** science-faith-dialogue website, "Convergences & Divergences" page
**Date:** March 2026 | **Status:** Phase 1 — Literature Research (Working Draft)

### Methodology Note

This reference file maps genuine convergences, divergences, and productive tensions between Buddhist and Christian philosophical traditions, with particular attention to the dialogue between **Christian Personalism** (Valčo's framework, drawing on Mounier, Wojtyła, and Trinitarian anthropology) and **Vietnamese Buddhist philosophy** (especially Nguyen Hoang Hai's work on dependent origination and emptiness).

The governing methodology is **colligation** (McGrath 2019, drawing on Whewell): bringing together insights from across intellectual traditions without reducing any one perspective to another. Each tradition retains its distinctive identity. We are not syncretizing; we are mapping where genuine convergences exist, where traditions honestly differ, and where productive tensions open space for further inquiry.

**Source selection criteria:** Peer-reviewed articles and monographs, with priority given to journals such as *Buddhist-Christian Studies*, *Zygon*, *Theological Studies*, the *Journal of Buddhist Ethics*, and *International Journal for Philosophy of Religion*; major monographs from scholars recognized in the field (Schmidt-Leukel, Abe, Cobb, Makransky, Knitter, Panikkar); and the specific source texts of the Valčo–Nguyen dialogue (McGrath 2019; Nguyen 2025; Valčo 2026 TNTI lecture).

**A note on verification:** Every citation below has been cross-referenced via web search. Where I was unable to independently verify a source's existence, I have marked it with [⚠ unverified — check before citing]. Sources without such markers have been confirmed.

## A. Ontology & Metaphysics

### Convergences

**1. Relational constitution of reality**
Both traditions affirm that reality is fundamentally relational rather than composed of isolated, self-subsistent substances. In Buddhist philosophy, *pratītyasamutpāda* (dependent origination) teaches that all phenomena arise in dependence on causes and conditions — nothing exists independently (Nguyen 2025, Ch. 1.3.1). In Christian theology, the Trinitarian understanding of God as an eternal communion of Persons (Father, Son, Spirit) grounds the claim that relationality is constitutive of being itself. As Grenz (2001) argues, the *imago Dei* is best understood not as a property of isolated individuals but as the relational character of human existence, rooted in a relational God.
— **Sources:** Nguyen Hoang Hai, *The Emptiness of Everything* (VNU Press, 2025), pp. 50–56; Stanley J. Grenz, *The Social God and the Relational Self: A Trinitarian Theology of the Imago Dei* (Westminster John Knox, 2001); Valčo 2026 TNTI lecture, "Gathering the Threads" section.
— **Presentation link:** Act II (Fork Diagram), Act V (closing convergence).

**2. Critique of the isolated, autonomous self**
Both traditions reject the atomized individual of Western liberal modernity. Nguyen's Buddhist-Marxist critique of neoliberal individualism (Nguyen 2025, pp. 282–284) converges with the Christian Personalist critique of Cartesian and Lockean individualism. The self is constituted through relationships, not prior to them. This shared platform provides a foundation for joint ethical engagement with AI, transhumanism, and consumerist culture.
— **Sources:** Nguyen 2025, pp. 282–284; Emmanuel Mounier, *Personalism* (Notre Dame Press, 1952/2001); Karol Wojtyła, *The Acting Person* (Reidel, 1979); Valčo 2026 TNTI lecture, "Gathering the Threads."

**3. Resistance to pure materialism/reductionism**
Both traditions resist the reduction of reality to what is measurable by the natural sciences. Nguyen acknowledges the limits of reason through his treatment of Gödel's incompleteness theorems and the irreducibility of contemplative experience (Nguyen 2025, pp. 157–159). Christian theology, following Torrance and McGrath, insists that reality is stratified and that different strata demand different investigative methods — physics does not "control" the mind but "enables" it (McGrath 2019, pp. 65–68, drawing on Bhaskar's critical realism).
— **Sources:** McGrath 2019, Ch. 2; Nguyen 2025, pp. 157–159; Thomas F. Torrance, *Theological Science* (OUP, 1969).

### Genuine Divergences

**4. Creation ex nihilo vs. beginninglessness**
The deepest ontological divergence. Christianity affirms that the world was created by a personal God from nothing (*creatio ex nihilo*), giving the cosmos its intelligibility, contingency, and moral order. Buddhism rejects or brackets the question of a First Cause; the Buddha himself famously refused to speculate on ultimate origins (the "poisoned arrow" parable), and Madhyamaka philosophy argues more explicitly that dependent origination entails no ultimate beginning — all things arise from causes and conditions without requiring a prime mover (Payutto 1994). Schmidt-Leukel (2006) devotes an entire edited volume to this question, asking whether it constitutes "the unbridgeable gulf" between the traditions. His answer is nuanced: Buddhist critiques of the creator-God concept target specific versions of theism (especially anthropomorphic, coercive models) that many Christian theologians also reject.
— **Sources:** Perry Schmidt-Leukel (ed.), *Buddhism, Christianity and the Question of Creation: Karmic or Divine?* (Ashgate, 2006) — especially Schmidt-Leukel's essay "Towards a Buddhist-Christian Theology of Creation"; P.A. Payutto, *Dependent Origination: The Buddhist Law of Conditionality* (Buddhadhamma Foundation, 1994); Valčo 2026 TNTI lecture, Act I.

**5. Contingency vs. emptiness (śūnyatā)**
Christian theology (Torrance, Polkinghorne) understands the world as *contingent* — it did not have to exist, and its specific structures could have been otherwise, which is why empirical observation is indispensable. This contingency is grounded in the free act of a Creator. Buddhist emptiness, by contrast, asserts that things lack inherent existence not because they were created but because they are composites of interdependent, impermanent factors (Nguyen's "two-property test": compositionality + impermanence = emptiness). Both concepts yield epistemological humility, but from different metaphysical foundations.
— **Sources:** Nguyen 2025, Preface (p. 4) and Ch. 1.3; John Polkinghorne, *Science and Creation* (SPCK, 1988); Thomas F. Torrance, *Divine and Contingent Order* (OUP, 1981); McGrath 2019, Ch. 8.

**6. Substance metaphysics vs. process/relational metaphysics**
Classical Christian theology (drawing on Aristotelian-Thomistic categories) speaks of substance, essence, and nature. Buddhist philosophy — especially Madhyamaka — systematically deconstructs substance categories: there are no self-subsistent essences, only interdependent processes. Process theology (Whitehead, Cobb, Griffin) has attempted to bridge this gap from the Christian side, but not without controversy. Cobb's dialogue with Abe explored whether process categories could provide shared philosophical ground.
— **Sources:** John B. Cobb Jr. and Christopher Ives (eds.), *The Emptying God: A Buddhist-Jewish-Christian Conversation* (Orbis, 1990); David Ray Griffin, *Unsnarling the World-Knot* (University of California Press, 1998); Nāgārjuna, *Mūlamadhyamakakārikā* (see Siderits & Katsura trans., 2013).

### Opportunities for Dialogue

**7. The "emptiness of emptiness" and the limits of ontological language**
Nāgārjuna's claim that emptiness itself is empty (*śūnyatā-śūnyatā*) — that it is a method of contemplation, not a metaphysical substance — resonates with the Christian apophatic tradition's insistence that God transcends all positive predication. Both moves prevent the absolutization of any conceptual framework. The Valčo–Nguyen dialogue can explore whether Nguyen's treatment of *śūnyatā-śūnyatā* (Nguyen 2025, pp. 70–73) and the Christian understanding of God as exceeding both existence and non-existence (Pseudo-Dionysius, *Mystical Theology*) represent genuinely analogous intellectual operations.
— **Sources:** Nguyen 2025, pp. 70–73; Pseudo-Dionysius, *Mystical Theology* (5th–6th c.); Hajime Nakamura's comparison of Dionysius and Nāgārjuna's Madhyamika (cited in Ford 2020 and Abe's works); McGrath 2019, Ch. 7.

## B. The Human Person / Self

### Convergences

**8. The self as relational, not self-enclosed**
Both traditions reject the Cartesian *cogito* as an adequate account of personhood. The Buddhist analysis of the five *skandhas* shows the "self" to be a composite of changing physical and mental factors with no permanent substrate. Christian Personalism (especially the relational turn in Trinitarian anthropology) argues that personhood is constituted *through* relationship — the child becomes a person through the mother's love before the child can even recognize it. Amos Yong (2005) demonstrates how nonreductive physicalist accounts of personhood emerging from neuroscience both contribute to and are enriched by the Christian concept of *pneuma* and the Buddhist concept of *pratītyasamutpāda*.
— **Sources:** Amos Yong, "Christian and Buddhist Perspectives on Neuropsychology and the Human Person: Pneuma and Pratītyasamutpāda," *Zygon* 40.1 (2005): 143–165; Valčo 2026 TNTI lecture, Act III; Nguyen 2025, Ch. 5 (consciousness as operating system).

**9. Epistemological humility about self-knowledge**
Both traditions recognize that consciousness is not self-transparent. Nguyen demonstrates that consciousness is a *model* of reality, not reality itself — the brain constructs "appropriate" rather than "truthful" representations (Nguyen 2025, Ch. 5). Paul's "now we see through a glass, darkly" (1 Cor 13:12) expresses a parallel epistemological modesty, though grounded in the eschatological hope that we will one day "see face to face."
— **Sources:** Nguyen 2025, Ch. 5; 1 Corinthians 13:12; McGrath 2019, Ch. 7 (mystery as trans-rational).

### Genuine Divergences

**10. Imago Dei vs. anattā**
The sharpest anthropological divergence. Christianity affirms that human beings bear the *imago Dei* — an indelible dignity, a relational vocation, and a moral agency grounded in being created in God's image. Buddhism teaches *anattā* (no-self): there is no permanent, unchanging essence to the person; what we call "self" is a conventional designation for an ever-changing process of interdependent factors. (It should be noted that Buddhism is not monolithic on this point: the Pudgalavādin tradition argued for a kind of person irreducible to the skandhas, and East Asian tathāgatagarbha or "Buddha-nature" traditions develop accounts that some scholars read as functionally affirming an enduring spiritual nature.) The Buddhist concept of the equality of all sentient beings extends compassion universally but, as recent scholarship notes, risks diminishing individual personality and dignity — a value that emerges precisely from the Christian *imago Dei* tradition (see the 2024 MDPI article by Lu et al. on Buddhist-Christian self/other dialogue).
— **Sources:** Lu et al., "The Self and the Other: A Further Reflection on Buddhist-Christian Dialogue," *Religions* 15.3 (2024): 376; Nicholson, "The Spirit of Contradiction in the Buddhist Doctrine of Not-Self," *Journal of Religion* 92.1 (2012): 31–57; Grenz 2001; Metanexus conference paper, "Personhood or Non-Self" (2008).

**11. Moral agency: grounded in divine image vs. emergent from dependent origination**
If there is no permanent self, what grounds moral responsibility? Buddhist ethics addresses this through the doctrine of karma and the conventional reality of the person (the "chariot" analogy in the *Milindapañha*). Christian ethics grounds moral agency in the *imago Dei*: the human person, as a finite but real agent created by God, bears genuine responsibility for choices. Makransky (2014) argues that Buddhist and Christian ethical epistemologies each have blind spots correctible through dialogue — Buddhist emphasis on inner psychological causes of suffering complements Christian liberation theology's focus on systemic oppression.
— **Sources:** John Makransky, "What Christian Liberation Theology and Buddhism Need to Learn from Each Other," *Buddhist-Christian Studies* 34 (2014): 117–134; Peter Harvey, *An Introduction to Buddhist Ethics* (Cambridge UP, 2000); Valčo 2026 TNTI lecture, closing question to Nguyen.

### Opportunities for Dialogue

**12. Can relational personhood bridge imago Dei and dependent arising?**
Both traditions affirm that humans are constituted through relationships. The question is whether dependent origination's account of relational constitution can accommodate the irreducible dignity and moral agency that Christian Personalism claims, and conversely, whether Christian Personalism can learn from Buddhism's more radical decentering of the ego without losing the person. Yong's (2005) proposal of a pneumatological anthropology informed by codependent origination represents one serious attempt.
— **Sources:** Yong 2005; Grenz 2001; Makransky 2014.

## C. Soteriology & Liberation

### Convergences

**13. The diagnosis of the human condition as distorted**
Both traditions agree that something has gone fundamentally wrong with the human condition. Christianity names this *sin* (*hamartia* — literally, "missing the mark"): alienation from God, self, neighbor, and creation. Buddhism names it *avidyā* (ignorance) and *taṇhā* (craving/thirst): the root cause of suffering is a fundamental misperception of reality, leading to grasping and attachment. McGrath's colligation model (2019, pp. 218–221) demonstrates how evolutionary biology's account of selfish predispositions, cultural anthropology's narrative identity, and the Christian-Buddhist diagnosis can be woven together without flattening any of them.
— **Sources:** McGrath 2019, pp. 218–221; Nguyen 2025, Ch. 1.3.2 (suffering); Valčo 2026 TNTI lecture, Act III.

**14. The transformative path requires discipline, community, and self-transcendence**
Both traditions prescribe a path that demands moral transformation, sustained practice, and communal support. The Christian life of faith involves *ekklesia* (church), sacraments, prayer, and progressive sanctification. The Buddhist path involves *saṅgha* (community), meditation, ethical precepts (*śīla*), and progressive awakening. Neither tradition treats liberation as instantaneous or purely intellectual.
— **Sources:** Harvey 2000; Valčo 2026 TNTI lecture, Act IV.

### Genuine Divergences

**15. Salvation (sōtēria) vs. nirvāṇa**
Christian salvation involves the restoration of relationship with a personal God, culminating in the resurrection of the body and eternal communion. Buddhist liberation (nirvāṇa) involves the cessation of craving, the extinguishing of the fires of greed, hatred, and delusion, and release from the cycle of rebirth (*saṃsāra*). The telos is fundamentally different: restored relationship (Christianity) vs. cessation of suffering through insight (Buddhism). The eschatological dimension is particularly divergent: Christianity affirms the resurrection of the body; Buddhism teaches rebirth cycles from which one seeks liberation.
— **Sources:** Schmidt-Leukel 2006; David Loy, "Enlightenment in Buddhism and Advaita Vedanta: Are Nirvana and Moksha the Same?" *International Philosophical Quarterly* 22.1 (1982): 65–74.

**16. Grace vs. self-effort (with the Shin Buddhist exception)**
Christianity teaches that salvation is fundamentally a gift of divine grace — humans cannot save themselves. Buddhism generally emphasizes the practitioner's own effort along the path. The significant exception is Pure Land (Shin) Buddhism, where Shinran teaches that the practitioner's effort is insufficient and that liberation depends on the "Other Power" (*tariki*) of Amida Buddha's compassionate vow. This structural parallel with the Protestant doctrine of *sola gratia* has been extensively explored.
— **Sources:** Abe's biography and early Pure Land faith (see Wikipedia entry on Masao Abe); Alfred Bloom, *Shinran's Gospel of Pure Grace* (University of Arizona Press, 1965); Schmidt-Leukel 2017.

### Opportunities for Dialogue

**17. Kenosis and dynamic śūnyatā**
Masao Abe's landmark essay "Kenotic God and Dynamic Śūnyatā" (1984/1990) proposed that Paul's kenosis hymn (Philippians 2:5–11) — Christ's self-emptying — reveals a structural analogy with Buddhist emptiness. If God the Son empties himself, Abe asked, does this not imply a self-emptying in God's very nature? Christian respondents (Moltmann, Küng, Tracy, Cobb) found the proposal profound but problematic: Küng insisted the Philippians hymn speaks of the kenosis of Christ, not of God the Father. Nevertheless, the kenosis–śūnyatā dialogue remains one of the most productive sites for Buddhist-Christian philosophical encounter.
— **Sources:** Masao Abe, "Kenotic God and Dynamic Śūnyatā," in Cobb and Ives (eds.), *The Emptying God* (Orbis, 1990), 3–65; Christopher Ives (ed.), *Divine Emptiness and Historical Fullness* (Trinity Press, 1995); Cabanne, "Beyond Kenosis: New Foundations for Buddhist-Christian Dialogue," *Buddhist-Christian Studies* 13 (1993): 103–117; Botz-Bornstein, "Kenosis, Dynamic Śūnyatā and Weak Thought: Abe Masao and Gianni Vattimo," *Asian Philosophy* 25.4 (2015).

## D. Ethics & Compassion

### Convergences

**18. Self-giving love as the apex of the moral life**
Both traditions identify self-transcending love/compassion as the highest ethical reality. In Christianity, *agapē* — disinterested, unconditional love modeled on God's love for humanity — is the fulfillment of the moral law (Romans 13:8–10). In Mahāyāna Buddhism, *karuṇā* (compassion) coupled with *mettā* (loving-kindness) motivates the Bodhisattva's vow to delay personal nirvāṇa for the sake of all sentient beings. Despite arising from different metaphysical frameworks — *anattā* and *imago Dei* — both traditions demand the relinquishing of self-centered interests: the Bodhisattva relinquishes nirvāṇa for others, while *caritas* calls individuals to love others as God loves humanity. Both hold self-surrendering love as the apex of spiritual ethics.
— **Sources:** Makransky, *Awakening Through Love* (Wisdom, 2007); Hanayama, "Christian 'Love' and Buddhist 'Compassion'" in *Journal of Indian and Buddhist Studies* 20.1; Kreeft, "Comparing Christianity and Buddhism" (peterkreeft.com).

**19. Ethics of interdependence and ecological responsibility**
Both traditions ground ecological responsibility in the interconnectedness of all life. Nguyen's application of dependent origination to environmental ethics (Nguyen 2025, pp. 282–284) and Catholic Social Teaching's concept of integral ecology (Pope Francis, *Laudato Si'*, 2015) converge on the claim that ecological destruction follows from the illusion of human separateness from nature.
— **Sources:** Nguyen 2025; Pope Francis, *Laudato Si'* (2015); John D'Arcy May, "Buddhists, Christians and Ecology," in Schmidt-Leukel (ed.) 2006.

### Genuine Divergences

**20. Love as constitutive reality vs. love as skillful means**
The sharpest ethical divergence — and arguably the sharpest divergence in the entire dialogue. If the self is ultimately illusory, then love between persons is, at bottom, a skillful means for alleviating suffering — functionally real but ontologically empty. If the self is a genuine, God-given person, then love is constitutive of reality itself — a participation in the eternal love of the triune God. This is the "What happens to love?" question that closes the Valčo presentation. Nguyen himself does not draw this conclusion explicitly, but his framework — taken to its logical end — implies it: if consciousness is a purposeless operating system and the self an adaptive construction, then the mother's love for her child is ultimately a dopamine-mediated survival mechanism rather than a genuine participation in something transcendent.
— **Sources:** Valčo 2026 TNTI lecture, closing; Nguyen Reading Notes, Section E.5 and Closing Observation; Peter Kreeft, "Comparing Christianity and Buddhism" (online essay at peterkreeft.com).

### Opportunities for Dialogue

**21. Virtue ethics as common ground: pāramitā and Aristotelian-Thomistic virtues**
Both traditions have developed sophisticated virtue-ethical frameworks. The Buddhist *pāramitās* (perfections — generosity, patience, ethical conduct, meditative concentration, wisdom, etc.) and the Christian cardinal and theological virtues (prudence, justice, temperance, fortitude + faith, hope, love) share a structural pattern: virtues are acquired through practice, shape the character of the practitioner, and orient the person toward a transcendent goal. Comparative work on this axis remains underdeveloped.
— **Sources:** Damien Keown, *The Nature of Buddhist Ethics* (Palgrave, 2001); Charles Goodman, *Consequences of Compassion: An Interpretation and Defense of Buddhist Ethics* (OUP, 2009); Alasdair MacIntyre, *After Virtue* (Notre Dame Press, 1981).

## E. Epistemology & Mysticism

### Convergences

**22. The apophatic tradition and śūnyatā**
Perhaps the deepest epistemological convergence. The Christian apophatic tradition (Gregory of Nyssa, Pseudo-Dionysius, Meister Eckhart, *The Cloud of Unknowing*) insists that God ultimately transcends all positive predication — the highest knowledge of God is a "knowing by not knowing," an entry into "the brilliant darkness of a hidden silence" (Pseudo-Dionysius, *Mystical Theology*, Ch. 1). Buddhist śūnyatā similarly asserts that ultimate reality cannot be captured by conceptual categories. Hajime Nakamura compared Dionysius's "super-essential Darkness" with Nāgārjuna's "void," going so far as to say certain passages of the *Mystical Theology* could be called a Christian version of the Heart Sutra. The Kyoto School (Nishida, Nishitani, Abe) built an entire philosophical program on exploring these parallels.
— **Sources:** Pseudo-Dionysius, *Mystical Theology* (5th–6th c.); Hajime Nakamura (cited in James Ford, "The Zen Priest Considers Dionysius," *Patheos*, 2020); Masao Abe, *Zen and Western Thought* (University of Hawaii Press, 1985); Catherine Keller, *Cloud of the Impossible* (Columbia UP, 2014); McGrath 2019, Ch. 7 (mystery as trans-rational); Nguyen 2025, pp. 70–73.

**23. Contemplative practice as epistemic pathway**
Both traditions affirm that certain truths are accessible not through discursive reasoning alone but through disciplined contemplative practice. Christian hesychasm, Ignatian contemplation, Carmelite prayer, and lectio divina all share with Buddhist *vipassanā*, *śamatha*, and Zen *zazen* the claim that stilling the discursive mind opens access to deeper dimensions of reality. Makransky's work on Sustainable Compassion Training explicitly designs contemplative practices accessible across traditions.
— **Sources:** Makransky, *Awakening Through Love* (Wisdom, 2007); Makransky & Condon, *How Compassion Works* (2025); Thomas Keating, *Open Mind, Open Heart* (Continuum, 1986).

### Genuine Divergences

**24. The apophatic difference: superabundant Being vs. dissolution of subject-object distinction**
The convergence between apophatic theology and śūnyatā has real limits. The Christian apophatic tradition anchors its negations in a *personal* God who exceeds human concepts precisely because God is *more* than what we can think — as superabundant Being, not as absence. Nāgārjuna's emptiness transcends both existence and non-existence — but not as a Subject encountering an object. As the Nguyen reading notes observe: "The Christian God transcends both [existence and non-existence] as well — but as *superabundant being*, not as the dissolution of the subject-object distinction. This is a genuine and deep difference."
— **Sources:** Nguyen Reading Notes, Section D; Vladimir Lossky, *The Mystical Theology of the Eastern Church* (1944/SVS Press, 1976); Hans Waldenfels, *Absolute Nothingness: Foundations for a Buddhist-Christian Dialogue* (Paulist Press, 1980).

**25. Revelation vs. awakening as epistemic models**
Christianity holds that the deepest truths about God, humanity, and salvation are *revealed* — they come to us from beyond, through Scripture, the person of Christ, and the Holy Spirit. Buddhism holds that the deepest truths are *awakened to* — they are discovered through the practitioner's own contemplative inquiry, following the Buddha's example. The epistemic direction differs: top-down gift (Christianity) vs. inside-out insight (Buddhism), though this distinction is less sharp than it first appears (the Buddhist concept of *saddhā* [trust/faith] plays a larger role than is often recognized, and the Christian emphasis on personal appropriation of faith through prayer and experience introduces a participatory element).
— **Sources:** Schmidt-Leukel 2017, Ch. 11–13; McGrath 2019, Ch. 8.

### Opportunities for Dialogue

**26. Faith (pistis) and trust (saddhā) — structural parallels**
While Christianity and Buddhism differ on the object of faith (personal God vs. the Dharma/Buddha's teaching), the *structure* of faith — as a trust that precedes and enables understanding, rather than resulting from it — shows striking parallels. Augustine's *crede ut intelligas* ("believe in order to understand") has a structural analog in the Buddhist teaching that *saddhā* (confident trust in the Buddha, Dharma, and Saṅgha) is a necessary condition for progress on the path, not a substitute for personal insight.
— **Sources:** McGrath 2019, Ch. 8; Harvey 2000, Ch. 1.

## F. Science & Philosophy of Science

### Convergences

**27. Anti-scientism: science is powerful but not omniscient**
Both traditions resist the claim that scientific method exhausts all legitimate ways of knowing. McGrath (2019), drawing on Kidd, identifies three scientistic "impulses" (imperialist, salvific, absolutist) that both Christian theology and Buddhist philosophy can critique. Nguyen explicitly acknowledges the limits of reason through his treatment of Gödel (Nguyen 2025, pp. 157–159). The Valčo lecture makes this a central theme: "To speak of the limits of science is not to weaken science. It is to clarify its excellence."
— **Sources:** McGrath 2019, Ch. 2; Nguyen 2025, pp. 157–159; Valčo 2026 TNTI lecture, Act IV.

**28. The ethical dimension of scientific inquiry**
Both traditions insist that knowledge is not morally neutral. Nguyen's warnings about neoliberalism, environmental destruction, and dopamine-overload culture flow from dependent origination and the doctrine of suffering (Nguyen 2025). The Christian tradition understands inquiry as *vocation* — ordered toward the good, accountable to moral norms. Both agree: science without ethical orientation becomes dangerous.
— **Sources:** Nguyen 2025, pp. 282–284; Valčo 2026 TNTI lecture, "Gathering the Threads"; Pope Francis, *Laudato Si'* (2015).

### Genuine Divergences

**29. Theology of nature vs. Buddhist naturalism**
Christian theology of nature (Torrance, McGrath, Polkinghorne) sees the intelligibility of the cosmos as grounded in the *Logos* — divine reason — and the contingency of creation as the reason empirical observation matters. Buddhist philosophy (Nguyen's approach) sees the same intelligibility as the self-organizing character of dependent origination — no external Logos required. The world is intelligible not because a Mind made it so, but because interdependent causation produces stable patterns. This divergence has direct implications for how each tradition approaches fine-tuning arguments and the anthropic principle.
— **Sources:** Valčo 2026 TNTI lecture, Act I; Nguyen 2025, Ch. 2–4; McGrath 2019, Ch. 8; Polkinghorne, *Science and Creation* (1988).

**30. Consciousness: theological anthropology vs. Buddhist mind-science**
Nguyen treats consciousness as an "operating system" empowered by DNA — a purposeless tool that nonetheless makes sophisticated modeling possible (Nguyen 2025, Ch. 5). Christian theological anthropology sees consciousness as an essential dimension of the *imago Dei*, oriented toward truth, beauty, and communion with God. The question of whether consciousness is *oriented toward truth* or merely toward *survival-appropriate modeling* is a frontier question for both AI ethics and the science-religion dialogue.
— **Sources:** Nguyen 2025, Ch. 5; Yong 2005; Evan Thompson, *Waking, Dreaming, Being: Self and Consciousness in Neuroscience, Meditation, and Philosophy* (Columbia UP, 2015); Nguyen Reading Notes, Section C.

### Opportunities for Dialogue

**31. AI ethics from both traditions**
Both traditions have resources for critiquing the assumption that artificial intelligence can replicate or replace human personhood. Buddhism's analysis of consciousness as dependently originated challenges the functionalist assumption that consciousness is substrate-independent. Christianity's *imago Dei* anthropology insists on an irreducible dignity that cannot be programmed. Joint work on this topic — already the focus of the Erasmus+ KA171 project — represents a frontier opportunity.
— **Sources:** Projektový zámer (Project Proposal), 2025; KA171 Partnership Questionnaire (VNU Hanoi); Valčo 2026 TNTI lecture; Nguyen 2025.
