## PART ONE: ANNOTATED OUTLINE OF THE BOOK'S ARCHITECTURE

---

## Preface — The Book's Thesis in a Nutshell

Nguyen states his core claim upfront: Buddhism and modern science "not only share commonalities but can also complement and support each other." The book limits itself to three Buddhist principles — **dependent origination**, **suffering**, and the **middle way** — and then shows these reflected in four scientific domains: the material world, life, the human body, and consciousness.

The argumentative strategy is distinctive. Nguyen does not simply *assert* resonances between Buddhism and science. He constructs a **two-property test for emptiness**: if any thing or phenomenon can be shown to exhibit (1) **compositionality** (it is made of parts, and its properties arise from the interaction of those parts) and (2) **impermanence** (those interactions are constantly changing), then that thing possesses *emptiness* — it has no self-existent, independent, fixed essence. The rest of the book applies this test, domain by domain, from subatomic particles to human consciousness.

**Key claim (Preface, p. 4):** "Proving that things and phenomena have compositional nature and impermanence means that things and phenomena have emptiness."

This is the book's methodological backbone. Everything else is illustration and application.

## Chapter 1: Principles

### 1.1–1.2 Indian Civilization and History of Buddhism (pp. 10–48)

These sections provide historical context — plate tectonics forming the Himalayas, the Indus Valley civilization, Aryan migration, caste formation, and the Buddha's emergence as a reformer who broke down caste distinctions and eliminated deity worship. Nguyen traces the development of Buddhism from Theravada through Mahayana to Vietnamese Zen (Truc Lam), positioning his book within a Vietnamese intellectual tradition that runs from King Tran Nhan Tong to the present.

**Notable move:** Nguyen emphasizes that Buddhism is "more philosophical than superstitious" (Ch. 1 Summary), positioning it as a rational philosophical system rather than a faith tradition. This framing choice will matter for dialogue — it signals the kind of Buddhism he is working with.

### 1.3 Principles of Buddhism — The Philosophical Core (pp. 49–72)

This is the foundational section for the entire book. Three principles are presented, but they are not independent — they are mutually constitutive.

#### 1.3.1 Dependent Origination (*pratītya-samutpāda*) (pp. 50–56)

**Main thesis:** All things exist in causal relationships; nothing exists independently. Dependent origination is the foundational principle from which everything else — no-self, emptiness, suffering — follows.

**Key philosophical moves:**

- Nguyen contrasts the Upanishadic **Self Theory** (permanent, unchanging *ātman* identical with *brahman*) with the Buddhist **No-Self Theory** (no permanent, independent soul; all things always transform). This is presented in a clear table (Table 1.1, p. 51).
- The Buddha's formulation from the Majjhima-nikāya (MN 3.63): "Because this exists, that exists; because this arises, that arises."
- **Nagarjuna's expansion** is critical: the Madhyamaka school moved dependent origination from a principle about sentient beings to an **ontological principle about all dharmas in the universe** — "no things exist independently, and instead, all dharmas exist through relationships."
- **Tsongkhapa's three levels** of dependent origination: (1) causal dependence (causes and conditions → impermanence in time), (2) whole–part dependence (compositionality in space), (3) dependence on conceptual imputation (dependent origination in mind). Nguyen uses levels (1) and (2) for scientific application, setting aside (3) as "subjective."
- The **three marks of existence**: impermanence, no-self, and Nirvana — presented as closely related consequences of dependent origination.

**Bold claim:** Nguyen explicitly compares Buddhist emptiness to "the principle of universal interconnection in Marx's philosophy" (p. 56) — a move that locates him within Vietnamese Marxist-Buddhist intellectual culture. This is not incidental; it shapes his entire interpretive framework.

#### 1.3.2 Suffering (*dukkha*) (pp. 56–59)

Three types of suffering are identified:
1. **Physical suffering** — birth, aging, sickness, death
2. **Mental suffering** — the hedonic treadmill: pleasures quickly fade, desires replace desires
3. **Suffering from ignorance** — the most subtle: not recognizing that the entire world is impermanent

The Four Noble Truths and Noble Eightfold Path are presented as the diagnostic and therapeutic framework. The second Noble Truth (origin of suffering) includes a noteworthy point: "mere belief is not enough but must also understand, verify, and practice" — Nguyen presents Buddhism as empirically oriented, not dogmatic.

#### 1.3.3 The Middle Way (*madhyamā-pratipad*) (pp. 59–62)

The middle way is presented at two levels:
- **Southern Buddhism:** Avoiding extremes of hedonism and asceticism; practicing the Eightfold Path
- **Northern Buddhism (Madhyamaka):** Avoiding the extremes of eternalism (things are permanent) and annihilationism (things are nothing) — a profound philosophical system about the nature of reality itself

**Scientific application:** Nguyen interprets the middle way as **optimization**: "the principle of least action" in physics and "fitness" in evolution are manifestations of the middle way. This is a creative but contestable move — it risks flattening a soteriological principle into a description of physical optimization.

#### 1.3.4 Emptiness (*śūnyatā*) (pp. 62–70) — **CRITICAL SECTION**

**Main thesis:** Emptiness = the absence of a self-nature, independent, unchanging nature in all things and phenomena.

**Key philosophical moves:**
- From the Buddha's **no-self** (applied to persons) to Nagarjuna's **emptiness** (applied to all dharmas) — this is "very significant development."
- The Madhyamaka school **equates dependent origination with emptiness**: "emptiness is dependent origination; dependent origination is emptiness."
- Nagarjuna's "eight negations" (Madhyamaka Treatise 1.1): "Neither arising nor ceasing, neither permanent nor annihilated, neither one nor different, neither coming nor going."
- The Yogacara (consciousness-only) school is acknowledged but criticized for overemphasizing consciousness. Nguyen explicitly uses the **Madhyamaka** concept of emptiness.

**The two-property test (pp. 67–68):** This is the book's methodological innovation. To prove emptiness in any domain:
1. **Compositionality** = emptiness in space (things are composed of parts; their properties arise from part-interactions)
2. **Impermanence** = emptiness in time (interactions between parts constantly change)

Nguyen sets Planck length (1.6 × 10⁻³⁵ m) and Planck time (5.4 × 10⁻⁴⁴ s) as the theoretical lower bounds of compositionality and impermanence, acknowledging that human experimental capabilities are far from touching those limits.

#### 1.3.5 Emptiness of Emptiness (*śūnyatā-śūnyatā*) (pp. 70–73) — **CRITICAL SECTION**

This is the most philosophically demanding part of the book. If everything is empty, is *emptiness itself* empty?

**Key philosophical moves:**
- Nguyen recognizes this as a **self-referential paradox**, analogous to the liar's paradox, Russell's paradox, and Gödel's incompleteness.
- He presents **two solutions**: (1) The Western logical solution (Russell, Tarski) — push the paradox outside the language by stratifying meta-languages. (2) **Nagarjuna's radically different solution** — refuse the subject–object division that creates the paradox in the first place. Emptiness is not a *thing* that knows or is known; it is a "method of contemplation that is meta-ontological and meta-epistemological."
- **Bold claim:** "Nagarjuna does not reduce emptiness to ontology, nor does he identify emptiness with knowledge, but views emptiness as a method of contemplation... Emptiness is not what is known, nor is it the knower, but is the dissolution of both in the insight of dependent origination."
- The middle way "does not mean between two poles but transcends the entire field of opposites."

**Friction point:** This section reveals the deepest tension between Nguyen's Buddhist framework and a Christian realist metaphysics. For Nagarjuna, the very question "what exists?" is "structurally wrong." For a Christian ontology, it is the most important question one can ask.

## Chapter 2: Matter (pp. 79–162)

### 2.1–2.2 Atoms, Quantum Mechanics, Standard Model (pp. 80–118)

Nguyen provides a lucid survey of modern physics. The key argument: at every level of examination, matter exhibits compositionality and impermanence.

**Key claims:**
- Fundamental particles (quarks, electrons, neutrinos) "are not unchanging and independent but continuously appear and disappear." Viewed from afar, they appear as particles with fixed properties; but according to quantum mechanics, they are "merely remnants of a continuous process of appearance and disappearance of countless particles in the vacuum."
- String theory pushes further: particles are composed of strings in 10–11-dimensional space, "realities completely different from human perception."
- Space and time "do not exist independently of occurring events but can expand, contract, and bend by matter within them."
- Wave-particle duality, superposition, uncertainty principle all show that "microscopic particles are closely related, one depending on and determining the other" — i.e., dependent origination at the physical level.

### 2.3 Time and Entropy (pp. 118–143)

Nguyen argues that the arrow of time is "assigned to" entropy increase (second law of thermodynamics). Time is not an independent entity but is linked to the directionality of thermodynamic processes. Space and time "co-exist because they depend on each other inseparably."

### 2.4 Information and the Holographic Principle (pp. 143–162) — **KEY SECTION**

**Main thesis:** Information may be the most fundamental category — more fundamental than matter or consciousness.

**Key philosophical moves:**
- **Susskind's "law −1"**: Information is always conserved. Entropy = hidden information. The Bekenstein-Hawking formula shows that black hole entropy is proportional to the *area* of the event horizon, not the volume — leading to the holographic principle.
- **The holographic principle** (Gerard 't Hooft, 1994; developed by Susskind and Maldacena): 3D information can be encoded on a 2D boundary surface. This implies that "3-dimensional space we sense may not be fundamental reality but only a representation of a deeper reality in lower-dimensional space."
- Nguyen explicitly draws the Buddhist parallel: "In Buddhism, there is a similar view: the phenomenal world we sense (relative truth) is only a manifestation of a deeper reality (ultimate truth)."
- **Spacetime as emergent:** Ryu-Takayanagi (2006) and Engelhardt (2015) showed that quantum entanglement in CFT creates spacetime structure in AdS. "Without quantum entanglement, there is no spacetime." Space is not fundamental but emergent from quantum entanglement in a lower-dimensional space.

**Bold claim:** "Spacetime is not fundamental reality; it is a consequence of deeper things: quantum entanglement." This aligns with emptiness: spacetime has no independent self-nature.

#### 2.4.5 Rational and Sensory Perception (pp. 157–162)

A philosophically rich section. Nguyen draws on:
- **Gödel's incompleteness theorem** — "wanting to explain everything by reason is impossible and relying only on reason cannot be complete." This is where "Buddhism can accompany science, can use personal experiences of meditation processes to help science understand truth."
- **Fujiwara Masahiko** — problems of humanity "cannot be solved only by reason but also need emotion and form."
- **Zen Master Huyen Quang** — the middle way combining reason and emotion, "clearly recognizing the dependent origination principle about mutual dependence of things to see emptiness everywhere to be able to behave appropriately, humbly with nature."

**Friction point:** Nguyen approvingly presents Gödel as evidence for the limits of reason — but then appeals to *meditation* and *emotion* rather than to a transcendent ground of intelligibility. A Christian philosopher would point to the *Logos* as the ground that makes reason both possible and limited.

## Chapter 3: Life (pp. 163–234)

The chapter covers the origin of life, evolution theory, genetics, epigenetics, and gene technology. The overarching argument: life, at every level — from molecular to organismal — exhibits compositionality and impermanence. Genes are the true "self-replicating subjects" of evolution; organisms are "living machines" genes use to replicate.

### 3.4 Buddhism with Life and Evolution (pp. 228–234) — **KEY SECTION**

**Key philosophical moves:**
- Nguyen addresses the apparent contradiction between the scientific origin of life (which has a temporal starting point) and the Buddhist doctrine of "beginningless, endless" reincarnation. He offers three reconciliations: (1) The Buddha's "beginningless" meant "very long relative to human lifetimes," not literally infinite. (2) At finer scales of observation, the boundary of "life" vs. "non-life" is blurry, like the boundary of an electron cloud. (3) Omniscience sees all processes as continuous causes and conditions; only limited observers see a "sudden" beginning.
- He argues that life "has no starting point; it continuously arises and ceases due to conditions, without a first cause." This is a **direct tension** with the Christian doctrine of *creatio ex nihilo*.
- Buddhism "easily accepted" Darwin because the Buddha held all organisms as equal — "Buddha is in the heart of all sentient beings."
- "Buddhism denies the self of things but instead argues about mutual causal relationships. According to causal relationships, self and world, body and mind are not divided but understood in mutual causal relationships."

**Bold claims to flag:**
- "Creation theory and evolution as a discussion about the beginning of existence is a wrong view" — Nguyen dismisses creationism *and* the metaphysical question of origins as a category error.
- Life is empty because it is composite and impermanent at every level (DNA, organism, ecosystem). No species "can exist independently, self-existent, and immutable."

## Chapter 4: Human (pp. 235–294)

### 4.1–4.3 The Human Body, Cells, and Genes (pp. 235–276)

Nguyen builds a relentless case that the human body is not a unified, self-contained entity but a **symbiotic ecosystem**:
- **Body level:** The human body hosts tens of trillions of microorganisms, mainly in the intestines. These microorganisms digest food, support the immune system, and assist the nervous system ("the second brain").
- **Cell level:** Most cells contain mitochondria — originally foreign bacteria that invaded billions of years ago and now serve as energy factories, timekeepers, and regulators of cell death. Chloroplasts play a similar role in plants. Recently, cyanelles have been discovered as bacteria *in the process of* becoming organelles in sea algae.
- **Gene level:** Only ~20% of human DNA encodes and controls proteins; the remainder consists of self-replicators of unknown function. The human genome is "a symbiotic ecosystem where genes cooperate and compete to exist and replicate."

### 4.4 Self (pp. 276–294) — **CRITICAL SECTION FOR DIALOGUE**

#### 4.4.1 Self is Relationships (pp. 276–282)

**Main thesis:** The self has no self-nature; it is constituted by relationships — with the surrounding world, with nature, with society, and with oneself.

**Key philosophical moves:**
- **Theseus's Ship Paradox:** The "Aegeus version" (continuously repaired, retains functional continuity) vs. the "Piraeus version" (rebuilt from old parts, retains material identity). Nguyen concludes that the *relational* identity — history of relationships with the world — is what constitutes the self, not material composition.
- **The psychological self is more important than the physical self** — memory, emotions, thinking, and relationships define "who I am." Physical matter is continuously replaced (~18 months for total body mass renewal), yet consciousness provides a sense of a "single and unchanging self." This feeling of permanence is itself the illusion.
- **Category Theory and Yoneda's Lemma:** Nguyen makes the remarkable claim that two entities are isomorphic if and only if their *relationships* with all other entities are isomorphic. Applied to persons: "twin brothers are still different because their relationships with themselves and with the external world are different."
- **Marx's formula enriched:** "Humans are the sum of relationships with the surrounding world, with nature and society, even with themselves." This includes three domains: nature, society, and thinking (self-relation).
- Value systems change faster than a human lifetime — creating intergenerational conflict and making the answer to "who am I?" depend on the historical moment.

#### 4.4.2–4.4.3 Placing Self Too High / Human Impact on the World (pp. 282–287)

A sharp **ethical critique**:
- **Neoliberalism** is identified as a dangerous ideology that "elevates individuals, elevates self-interest thought" — "completely opposite to human nature as a social species." Nguyen quotes Thatcher: "Economics is the method; the goal is to change the soul."
- Three consequences: increasing wealth gap, corporate domination, cultural self-interest.
- Wars are caused by "self-arrogance of individual selves of rulers, self-grandeur of a nation, or thinking that one's ideal is more correct than other ideals."
- Climate change data: Earth has warmed 1.3–2.3°C since the pre-industrial period; humanity has ~6 years of carbon budget at current emission rates.
- The solution: "mutual dependence on the human body and in Earth's ecosystem is much tighter than we think" → the middle way = "living harmoniously with nature."

#### 4.4.4 Power from Self Expansion (pp. 287–290)

Nguyen develops the concept of the **extended self** (after Russell Belk, 1988): tools, weapons, institutions, and now smartphones are extensions of the body and mind. The smartphone is "the most diverse expansion ever" — functional, anthropomorphic, and ontological (it affects us even when we don't want it to). AI integrated in smartphones represents a further expansion of causal power.

**Underlying argument:** If the self is constituted by relationships, and tools expand the network of relationships, then the self is *expandable* — and also vulnerable to manipulation through the expansion (social media addiction, AI-driven behavior modification).

#### 4.4.5 Illusion About the Self (pp. 291–294) — **CRITICAL SECTION**

**Main thesis:** The self's desires may not actually be "its own." Because the human body is an endosymbiotic ecosystem, and because ~80% of DNA consists of self-replicators that may be pursuing their own replication goals, individual "desires or cravings are not necessarily their desires but are the ecosystem's desires."

**Key philosophical moves:**
- The **T. gondii analogy:** Bacteria manipulate mice into fearlessly approaching cats (to continue the parasite's life cycle). The mice "are completely ignorant about their actions" and "think that is free will in their choice." Nguyen applies this to humans: our desires and actions may be "deeply controlled by genes through proteins" — making them "ignorant actions."
- **Consciousness as The Matrix:** "DNA can create consciousness like robots create The Matrix with two purposes: DNA hides its purposes and lets the body always be deluded that it is living for itself, not for the DNA master."
- The three-layered suffering: (1) fulfilling desires that are actually serving DNA, not the "self"; (2) not recognizing this manipulation; (3) recognizing it but being unable to escape. "The real escape is precisely escaping this body, escaping reincarnation."

**Bold claims:** This is the most provocative section. The self is an illusion crafted by DNA to keep the living machine compliant. Free will is illusory. "Could each person's self be an illusion that needs enlightenment to recognize?"

## Chapter 5: Consciousness (pp. 295–388)

### 5.1 Concepts of Consciousness (pp. 296–329)

#### 5.1.1 Neural Theories (pp. 296–298)

Nguyen surveys five schools of consciousness theory: materialism, idealism, dualism, panpsychism, and modern neural theory. He positions himself firmly in the **materialist** camp: "most scientists acknowledge consciousness is a consequence of physical and chemical activities of matter in the brain." Modern neural theories all hold that "consciousness is a model of the external world" — the brain as a "prediction machine" helping the body predict the future.

He acknowledges **quantum consciousness** (Penrose-Hameroff: quantum effects in microtubules) and notes a 2024 study showing quantum effects can occur at room temperature in neurons — though "how consciousness forms from those quantum effects still needs other research."

He also notes **Integrated Information Theory** (IIT) as combining materialist and panpsychist elements, and John Wheeler's "it from bit" — the hypothesis that information is the most fundamental category.

#### 5.1.2 The Brain is a Turing Machine (pp. 298–302) — **BOLD CLAIM**

**Main thesis:** "Brain activity is like a Turing machine." Nguyen justifies this "approximation" on two grounds: (1) brain activity is based on physics, chemistry, and biology (materialism); (2) neuron activities follow basic logic principles (binary logic gates). He acknowledges this excludes supernatural forces and that "emotions are also like that, all controlled by physical, chemical, and biological processes."

**Key data:**
- The brain operates at ~20 W but performs ~10¹⁸ operations per second (exaflop level) — the Oak Ridge Frontier supercomputer needs 20 MW for similar capability.
- ChatGPT-4 passed the Turing test in May 2024 (54% of interrogators identified it as human).

**Nguyen's qualification:** He acknowledges the philosophical zombie objection (Chalmers) but says "we temporarily accept the brain is a Turing machine in discussions in this book." The word *temporarily* is important — he is aware this is an approximation, not a demonstration.

#### 5.1.7 Consciousness as a Model of Reality (pp. 324–327)

Consciousness is a model the brain builds of the external world, "not simply acting according to gene control but tries to predict the future and sometimes even resists gene commands like Neo resists the robot world."

**Key argument from touch research (2021):** A congenitally touch-deprived person (Kim) and a person who lost touch in adulthood (Ian) both maintained body perception despite lacking the most fundamental sense. The brain can "self-perceive self, self-know self-existence without needing external assistance." This is presented as evidence against the "brain in a vat" hypothesis — suggesting "it is highly likely we are living in a real world, not a simulated world."

#### 5.1.8 Consciousness is the Operating System of the Body (pp. 327–330) — **KEY SECTION**

**Main thesis:** Consciousness is not simply a reward for living machines — it is the "immediate operating system" empowered to serve gene purposes. DNA sets goals (like a board of directors); consciousness executes them (like an executive board). But DNA "intentionally hides, does not let consciousness know about its control" — creating the illusion of free will.

**Key nuance:** Consciousness can sometimes *resist* DNA. Monks, priests, and clergy voluntarily abandon reproduction. The Pope's institutional continuity for 2000 years shows that "information has been inherited without needing through DNA." Max Tegmark's "Life 2.0" — human intelligence (software) can learn quickly and surpass genetic hardware.

**Bold claim:** "Consciousness is not an independent, self-determining entity but a tool, an operating system empowered to serve gene purposes." The emptiness of consciousness = consciousness has no self-nature; it is a product of evolutionary processes, contingent on brain structure.

### 5.2 The Senses and the Six Sense Bases (pp. 330–351)

Nguyen maps the Buddhist **six sense bases** (āyatana) onto modern neuroscience (Table 5.1, p. 342). The six roots: eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind; each with its corresponding dust (objects), consciousness, and physical substrate. The correspondence is remarkably detailed — he accounts for rhodopsin proteins in vision, hair cells in hearing, chemical receptors in taste and smell, and Piezo mechanical receptors in touch.

**Central insight:** All five physical senses operate through **electromagnetic interactions** and convert all signals into identical electrical pulses (~110 mV, ~3 ms). The senses perceive only "a very thin slice of reality." Humans can only sense electromagnetic force directly — not gravity, not nuclear forces.

### 5.4 Suffering and Happiness (pp. 362–388)

**5.4.1–5.4.2 Pain as Body Protection Mechanism (pp. 362–370):** Pain is a body protection system, a "damage signal," not intrinsically bad. Nguyen presents the neuroscience of nociception, gate control theory, and pain modulation. A congenital inability to feel pain is a "survival barrier" — such individuals rarely live past 25.

**5.4.3 Suffering Due to Dissatisfaction of Consciousness (pp. 370–376):** Here the book turns to Buddhist suffering in neuroscientific terms.
- **Pleasure as evolutionary bait:** "Pleasure is the reward of the future for individuals to overcome the attraction of the present." Dopamine is the key neurotransmitter — eating chocolate increases dopamine 55%, sex 100%, cocaine 225%, amphetamine 1000%.
- **The hedonic treadmill:** "Suffering and pleasure are processed by the same brain region" (Anna Lembke). The brain constantly recalibrates its dopamine baseline. Achieving pleasure raises the baseline; when stimulation stops, dopamine drops *below* the new baseline, producing suffering. This is the neuroscientific mechanism behind the Buddhist second suffering (dissatisfaction).
- **Homeostasis as middle way:** The brain seeks a dopamine balance state. "The middle way is manifested everywhere; excess, no matter which extreme, creates unpleasant experiences."

**5.4.4–5.4.6 Happiness and How to Achieve It (pp. 376–388):**
- **Pleasure ≠ Happiness:** Pleasure (dopamine) is strong, addictive, short-lived. Happiness (serotonin) is gentler, non-addictive, lasting. "Happiness is a mental state that most religions, including Buddhism, aim for."
- Modern society produces **dopamine overload** — easy access to stimuli that spike dopamine, leading to addiction cycles. "Humans once had to do many things to have an amount of dopamine creating pleasure in the past, but now can achieve that very easily." Social media, junk food, and stimulants are all dopamine traps.
- Nguyen argues for reducing input factors that spike dopamine (stimulants, social media, excessive consumption) and cultivating output factors (meditation, gratitude, meaningful relationships, physical exercise).

## Conclusion (pp. 389–394)

The Conclusion draws the threads together:

- **Ignorance** is the deepest suffering — "this entire book is only to show us suffering due to ignorance. Ignorance is the most core problem that Buddhism raises, understood as wrong awareness about reality."
- Consciousness evolved for **appropriateness, not truthfulness**: "most appropriately, not most truthfully."
- The anisotropy of entropy leads to the anisotropy of truth–goodness–beauty: "truth, goodness, beauty are not independent, self-existing values but they come from life."
- In all worlds — matter, life, body, consciousness — compositionality and impermanence are demonstrable. "These two properties lead to absolute truth: the emptiness of all things."
- The book closes with a poem by King Tran Nhan Tong: "Living in the world, joyful in the Way, follow conditions, / Hungry then eat, tired then sleep, / In the house there is treasure, stop searching, / Facing situations without mind, don't ask about meditation."

## PART TWO: POINTS FOR DIALOGUE — A CHRISTIAN PERSONALIST ENGAGEMENT

The following discussion points are organized for Prof. Valčo's conversations with Prof. Nguyen during the March 2026 Erasmus+ mobility at TNTI Hanoi. They are framed as *collegial engagement* — rigorous but warm, steelmanning Nguyen's positions before pressing on them.

---

### A. ONTOLOGY — WHAT IS REAL?

#### Where we converge:

Both traditions reject the idea that the world is a brute, self-explanatory given. Both insist that what *appears* to be solid, permanent, and self-sufficient is not. Nguyen's demonstration that matter, life, and consciousness are all composite and impermanent resonates with the Christian doctrine that all creatures are *contingent* — they do not contain the ground of their own being. Thomas Aquinas would recognize the impulse: no finite thing is *ens a se* (being from itself). In that sense, there is a real structural parallel between Buddhist emptiness and the Christian claim that creatures have no *independent* self-sufficiency. They depend — ultimately, for the Christian, on God; for Nguyen, on an endless web of causes and conditions.

McGrath's formula is useful here: we live in a world that is an **ontological unity** but must accept an **epistemological pluralism** in investigating it. Nguyen's Buddhist-scientific account and the Christian doctrine of creation are two epistemically situated perspectives on the same reality. The task is *colligation* — seeing what larger picture emerges — not adjudication.

#### Where we diverge — and the questions this opens:

1. **Contingency and the question of *why anything at all*:** Nguyen's emptiness is thoroughgoing: things arise from causes and conditions, which arise from further causes and conditions — and the chain has "no first point." But in the Christian philosophical tradition (and indeed in classical philosophy since Leibniz and Aquinas), the radical contingency of the world *points beyond itself* to a non-contingent ground. Compositionality and impermanence, on the Christian reading, are not self-explanatory — they are signs of createdness, of a world that *need not have existed*. The question is: Does Nguyen's framework have resources to address *why there is a web of dependent origination at all*, or does it treat this as a question that need not be asked? If the latter — is that a philosophical choice or a philosophical blind spot?

2. **Emptiness and the two truths:** Nguyen presents the Madhyamaka "two truths" — conventional truth (the world as we experience it) and ultimate truth (emptiness). But what exactly is the *ontological status* of emptiness? If emptiness is "absolute truth," it functions — despite protests to the contrary — as a kind of metaphysical ground. If it is merely a "method of contemplation" (as Nguyen says following Nagarjuna), then it has no ontological status at all, and the question reasserts itself: what *is* the ultimate nature of reality? The Christian answer is clear: reality is the gift of a personal Creator, and its contingency is intelligible precisely because it is willed. The Buddhist non-answer ("the question is structurally wrong") is philosophically sophisticated but may not be metaphysically stable.

3. **The holographic principle and levels of reality:** Nguyen's treatment of the holographic principle — that the 3D world we inhabit may be a "representation" of a deeper 2D reality — is fascinating. It resonates with McGrath's Bhaskarian critical realism: reality is *stratified*, with each level having its own integrity. But for McGrath and Bhaskar, the strata are *real* at each level, not illusory. Nguyen's tendency to describe our 3D experience as less "real" than the 2D quantum substrate risks a form of idealism — reducing experienced reality to a shadow of mathematical structure. A Christian realist would insist that the macroscopic world of persons, relationships, and moral action is *genuinely real*, not a mere epiphenomenon of quantum processes.

**Suggested conversation starter:** "Your demonstration that spacetime is emergent from quantum entanglement is remarkable. But I want to press on this: does the claim that the 2D substrate is 'more real' than the 3D world we inhabit amount to a new kind of reductionism — reducing experienced reality to mathematical structure? Or is there space in your framework for the lived, macroscopic world to be *genuinely real* in its own right?"

### B. THE SELF — WHO IS THE KNOWER?

This will be the sharpest point of divergence — and also, potentially, the most fruitful.

#### Steelmanning Nguyen's position:

Nguyen's case for the relational self is stronger than a superficial reading might suggest. He is not saying the self "doesn't exist" in some crude eliminativist sense. He is saying:
- The physical body is continuously renewed (complete matter replacement every ~18 months)
- At every level (body, cell, gene), humans are symbiotic ecosystems, not self-contained units
- The psychological self — memory, emotions, relationships — is more fundamental than the physical self
- The self is constituted by relationships (Yoneda's Lemma: identity = the totality of relationships)
- Value systems and self-understanding shift with historical context
- Therefore: the self has no *fixed, independent, unchanging* essence — which is precisely what emptiness means

This is a serious philosophical position, and a Christian Personalist should not dismiss it lightly. In fact, much of it *converges* with Christian relational anthropology.

#### The convergence — a shared critique of the isolated autonomous self:

Both Buddhist *anātman* and Christian Personalism reject the Cartesian–liberal picture of the self as an isolated, self-contained rational ego. For Nguyen, the self is constituted by relationships with nature, society, and thinking. For Christian Personalism (drawing on Trinitarian theology), the human person is *constitutively relational* — personhood emerges in the I-Thou encounter, grounded in the relational being of the triune God. The *imago Dei* is not a property of isolated individuals but of persons-in-relation. Luther's *simul iustus et peccator* also captures a non-static self, always "becoming" in the dynamic tension between sin and grace.

Nguyen's critique of neoliberalism as the elevation of the isolated self is something a Christian Personalist can wholeheartedly endorse. His invocation of Marx's "humans are the sum of social relationships" is, stripped of its materialist metaphysics, compatible with the Christian claim that we are made for communion.

#### The divergence — and why it matters:

1. **Constituted by relationships, but not *reducible* to them.** This is the crux. For Nguyen, the self *is* its relationships — nothing more. For Christian Personalism, the person is constituted *in and through* relationships but retains an irreducible *ipseity* — a "thisness" that is the gift of being called into existence by a personal God. The human person is an *imago Dei*: relational because God is relational (Trinity), but *real as a person* because God's creative act bestows genuine existence on each individual. Personhood is a gift, not an illusion.

   The test case is moral agency. If the self is *entirely* constituted by relationships and has no abiding center, who is the moral agent? Who bears responsibility? Who repents? Nguyen's framework has trouble accounting for genuine moral agency — the capacity not merely to *be shaped* by conditions but to *respond* to them as a free subject. The Christian claim is that this capacity is precisely what the *imago Dei* protects.

2. **The T. gondii problem — genes as puppet masters.** Nguyen's most provocative argument (pp. 291–294) suggests that human desires may be "ignorant actions" controlled by DNA, like parasite-infected mice rushing toward cats. This is a powerful image — but it is also self-undermining. If *all* human thought is ultimately gene-controlled illusion, then so is Nguyen's own argument. The book itself, his scientific reasoning, his Buddhist practice — all become mere epiphenomena of DNA pursuing replication. This is the classic self-defeat of thoroughgoing eliminativism. A Christian anthropology avoids this by grounding human rational capacity in the *Logos* — the divine reason that makes human reason possible and genuine.

3. **The question of vocation and calling.** If the self is illusory, what happens to the concept of *calling* — the idea that a person is summoned to a specific purpose, to a particular way of being in the world? Nguyen's framework can account for functional roles (DNA "assigns" the body certain tasks) but not for *vocation* in the thick sense — the experience of being addressed by another, of hearing a call that demands free response. This is central to both Luther's theology of Beruf and the broader Christian understanding of personhood as *responsive being*.

**Suggested conversation starter:** "I find your relational account of the self genuinely compelling — and it resonates with my own tradition more than you might expect. But I want to press on one point: if the self is *entirely* constituted by relationships and has no abiding center, who is the moral agent that can *take responsibility* for those relationships? In my framework, the person is relational but irreducible — the one who can say 'I' and mean it, who can be called by name and respond. Does your framework have resources for preserving genuine moral agency and responsibility?"

### C. CONSCIOUSNESS — IS IT COMPUTABLE?

#### Steelmanning Nguyen's position:

Nguyen is more careful than the section title "The Brain is a Turing Machine" might suggest. He explicitly calls this an "approximation" adopted "temporarily." He acknowledges Chalmers's hard problem, the philosophical zombie objection, and the unexplained gap between neural computation and subjective experience. His real claim is more modest: given our current understanding, treating brain activity as computational is the most productive working hypothesis for empirical science.

He also shows genuine awareness of limits: Gödel's incompleteness theorem demonstrates that "wanting to explain everything by reason is impossible." The appeal to meditation as a complement to reason (pp. 158–160) suggests he does not believe computation exhausts consciousness.

#### The tensions within his own framework:

1. **Materialism vs. Buddhist phenomenology.** Nguyen explicitly positions himself as a materialist about consciousness: it is "a consequence of physical and chemical activities of matter in the brain." But this sits uneasily with his Buddhist commitments. The Madhyamaka tradition (which he follows) holds that *all dharmas* are empty — including matter, including brain processes. If consciousness is produced by brain processes, and brain processes are themselves empty (composite, impermanent, without self-nature), then what is the *ontological status* of the consciousness that is "produced"? Is it empty too? If so, the very category "produced by" becomes unstable — since production presupposes real causal connections, and emptiness dissolves the fixity of causal connections.

   In other words: Nguyen's materialism about consciousness may be in tension with his Madhyamaka ontology. He needs consciousness to be *real enough* to be a model of reality and an operating system for the body, but *empty enough* to conform to Buddhist metaphysics. This is a productive tension to explore.

2. **The Turing machine claim and the hard problem.** Nguyen's claim that the brain is "like" a Turing machine is a functional description — it tells us what the brain *does* (computation), not what consciousness *is* (subjective experience). This is precisely Chalmers's point. A Christian anthropology, rooted in the irreducibility of the person, can accept that the brain *computes* without accepting that consciousness *just is* computation. The person is more than the mechanism — and this "more" is not a ghost in the machine but the *wholeness* of embodied personhood, the unity of a creature made in God's image.

3. **The "consciousness as operating system" metaphor and its limits.** Nguyen's claim that consciousness is an "operating system" empowered by DNA to run the body is a vivid metaphor — but it smuggles in assumptions. An operating system is *designed* by a designer. It has a *purpose* — efficient system management. It is *for* something. But if DNA is purposeless (as Nguyen explicitly states: "goals and plans that DNA gives are purposeless"), then consciousness is a purposeless operating system serving purposeless goals. This is coherent as a description, but it is also profoundly nihilistic — and it sits awkwardly with the Buddhist tradition's own soteriological claims about liberation, wisdom, and the cessation of suffering. If consciousness is just a purposeless tool, why does *enlightenment* matter?

**Suggested conversation starter:** "You call the brain a Turing machine, but you also say the brain's model of reality doesn't need to be *truthful* — only *appropriate.* I find this fascinating, because it means consciousness is not oriented toward truth but toward survival. But then I have to ask: is your *book* — which aims at truth about the nature of reality — itself just an 'appropriate' model for survival, or is it actually *true*? If your framework is right, how do we distinguish genuine insight from useful illusion? And does the Buddhist tradition's own claim to *wisdom* presuppose that consciousness can reach beyond mere adaptive modeling — toward something like truth?"

### D. SCIENCE AND ITS LIMITS — DOES NGUYEN RECOGNIZE THE TRANS-RATIONAL?

#### What Nguyen does well:

Nguyen explicitly acknowledges the limits of reason. His treatment of Gödel (pp. 157–159) is honest: "a scientific theory questioned to the end will have a proposition that we must accept cannot be proven true or false." His appeal to emotion, form, and meditation as complements to reason (Fujiwara, Huyen Quang) opens space beyond pure rationalism.

His treatment of the holographic principle also implicitly acknowledges that reality exceeds human perceptual and conceptual capacities — the "real" world may have more dimensions than we can access, and our experience is a thin slice of what exists.

#### Where the question presses:

1. **Is Nguyen's Buddhism genuinely non-reductive?** Despite his acknowledgment of reason's limits, Nguyen's operational method throughout the book is thoroughly scientific-materialist. Every chapter proceeds by showing that things can be decomposed into parts (compositionality) and that parts change (impermanence). This is a reductive procedure — and it works beautifully in physics, chemistry, and biology. But does it work for *meaning*? For *moral value*? For *beauty*? Nguyen mentions truth–goodness–beauty as arising from the anisotropy of entropy (p. 390), which is a reductive account: values *come from* life, which *comes from* atoms, which *come from* smaller particles. If this is the whole story, it is naturalism with Buddhist vocabulary.

   McGrath's anti-scientism argument (Kidd's three impulses: imperialist, salvific, absolutist) applies here. Does Nguyen's project exhibit the imperialist impulse — extending the compositionality-impermanence test into domains where its competency is "uncalibrated and almost certainly problematic"?

2. **The trans-rational in McGrath and Otto.** McGrath (drawing on Rudolf Otto) argues that the numinous is not irrational but *trans-rational* — it overwhelms and saturates rational capacities rather than contradicting them. Nguyen's Buddhism has resources for something similar: the experience of *enlightenment* is not reducible to propositional knowledge. But in practice, Nguyen's book treats everything as amenable to the compositionality-impermanence test. The trans-rational — whether as the numinous (Otto), the apophatic (Christian mysticism), or *satori* (Zen) — is acknowledged in passing but never given methodological weight.

3. **The "emptiness of emptiness" and the limits of language.** Nguyen's treatment of *śūnyatā-śūnyatā* (pp. 70–73) is the place where he comes closest to acknowledging a trans-rational dimension. Nagarjuna's claim that emptiness is "a method of contemplation that is meta-ontological and meta-epistemological" is, in its own way, an admission that the deepest reality exceeds both scientific measurement and conceptual articulation. This is where Buddhism and the Christian apophatic tradition (Pseudo-Dionysius, Meister Eckhart, Gregory of Nyssa) come closest to agreement: the *ultimate* is not fully captured by any positive description.

   But the Christian apophatic tradition anchors this in a *personal* God who exceeds human concepts precisely because God is *more* than what we can think — not *less*. Nagarjuna's emptiness transcends both existence and non-existence. The Christian God transcends both as well — but as *superabundant being*, not as the dissolution of the subject-object distinction. This is a genuine and deep difference, worth exploring together.

**Suggested conversation starter:** "Your treatment of Gödel and the limits of reason is compelling. But I notice that in practice, your book applies the compositionality-impermanence test quite universally — even to consciousness and values. I wonder: is there anything in your framework that *resists* being decomposed into parts and change? Something like what the Christian tradition calls 'mystery' — not irrationality, but the trans-rational, what overwhelms our conceptual capacity precisely because it is *more* than we can think?"

### E. CONVERGENCES WORTH CELEBRATING

These are areas where genuine agreement exists — and where the Valčo-Nguyen collaboration can bear fruit for the joint research project.

1. **The critique of the isolated autonomous self.** Both traditions see the atomized individual of Western liberal modernity as a distortion. Nguyen's Buddhist-Marxist critique of neoliberalism (pp. 282–284) and the Christian Personalist critique of Cartesian individualism converge powerfully. This is a shared platform for ethical engagement with AI, transhumanism, and consumerist culture. The self is *relational*, not self-enclosed.

2. **The relational character of reality.** Nguyen's demonstration that reality at every level — from quantum entanglement to symbiotic ecosystems to the neural networks of consciousness — is *relational* resonates with the Trinitarian insight that *being itself* is relational. The difference lies in the metaphysical grounding (dependent origination vs. Trinitarian communion), but the phenomenological observation is shared.

3. **Resistance to pure reductionism.** Despite the tensions noted above, Nguyen is not a crude reductionist. His insistence on the "middle way" between extremes, his acknowledgment of Gödel's incompleteness, his appeal to emotion and form alongside reason — all these open space for a non-reductive engagement with reality. The fact that he closes the book with poetry (Tran Nhan Tong) rather than equations is itself a philosophical statement.

4. **The ethical dimension of knowledge.** Nguyen's warning about neoliberalism, environmental destruction, and the dopamine-overload culture is not merely sociological — it flows from his understanding of dependent origination and suffering. Knowledge is not neutral; it has ethical implications. This converges with the Christian understanding of knowledge as *vocation* — inquiry as a calling ordered toward the good, as argued in the Valčo presentation at TNTI.

5. **The shared recognition that consciousness is not self-transparent.** Nguyen's demonstration that consciousness is a *model* of reality (not reality itself), that senses perceive only a thin electromagnetic slice, and that the brain builds "appropriate" rather than "truthful" representations resonates with the Christian humility about the limits of human knowledge. Paul's "now we see through a glass, darkly" (1 Cor 13:12) expresses a similar epistemological modesty — though grounded in the hope that one day we will "see face to face."

6. **The joint research framework.** McGrath's "colligation" provides the method: bringing together insights from Buddhist philosophy, Christian theology, and natural science without reducing any one perspective to the others. The Emptiness of Everything is exactly the kind of serious intellectual work that makes such colligation possible — because it takes both science and Buddhist philosophy seriously and presents them with clarity and rigor. The task now is to bring a third voice — the Christian Personalist voice — into the conversation, not as a judge but as a partner in inquiry.

### CLOSING OBSERVATION FOR THE DIALOGUE

Perhaps the deepest question Nguyen's book raises for a Christian is this: **What happens to love?** Nguyen's account of the self as relational, impermanent, and ultimately illusory is intellectually compelling — but it has no place for *love* as a constitutive reality. If the self is an illusion crafted by DNA, then love is too — just another adaptive strategy. But if love is *real* — if the mother's love for her child is not merely a dopamine-mediated survival mechanism but a genuine participation in something transcendent — then reality cannot be exhausted by compositionality and impermanence. Love points to a dimension of reality that is *relational but also personal, temporal but also eternal, vulnerable but also unconditional*.

The Christian claim is that this dimension is grounded in the love of a triune God — a God who is, in God's own being, an eternal communion of persons. The Buddhist will not accept this grounding. But the *phenomenon* of love — its irreducibility, its capacity to *create* personhood (as a mother's love creates the child's sense of self before the child can even recognize it) — is shared human experience. And it is, perhaps, the best starting point for a conversation between two friends who have thought deeply about what is real.
